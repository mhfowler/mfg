			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">List of proverbial phrases</h1>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>This is an alphabetical list of widely used and repeated proverbial phrases. Whenever known, the origin of the phrase or proverb is noted. The majority of these phrases can be found at one of the following resources:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-EnglishProverbs_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EnglishProverbs-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Putnam_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Putnam-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Punam_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Punam-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Proverbial_phrase" title="Proverbial phrase">proverbial phrase</a></b> or a <b><a href="/wiki/Proverbial_expression" class="mw-redirect" title="Proverbial expression">proverbial expression</a></b> is type of a conventional <a href="/wiki/Saying" title="Saying">saying</a> similar to <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">proverbs</a> and transmitted by oral tradition. The difference is that a proverb is a fixed <a href="/wiki/Expression_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Expression (language)">expression</a>, while a proverbial phrase permits alterations to fit the grammar of the context.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1768, John Ray defined a proverbial phrase as:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>A proverb [or proverbial phrase] is usually defined, an instructive sentence, or common and pithy saying, in which more is generally designed than expressed, famous for it peculiarity or elegance, and therefore adapted by the learned as well as the vulgar, by which it is distinguished from counterfeits which want such authority</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— John Ray, A Compleat Collection of English Proverbs,1798<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>

####A Lark bird in the hand is worth two in the bush####

####"A <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cat</a> may look at a <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">king</a>". Originates from the 16th century, and appeared in a political pamphlet in 1652. It refers to an impertinent comment made by someone of lower status (i.e. the cat looking) to someone with higher status (i.e. the king).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>####

####A <a href="/wiki/Chain" title="Chain">chain</a> is only as strong as its weakest link<sup id="cite_ref-EnglishProverbs_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EnglishProverbs-1">[1]</a></sup>####

</ul>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>In every chain of reasoning, the evidence of the last conclusion can be no greater than that of the weakest link of the chain, whatever may be the strength of the rest.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Reid" title="Thomas Reid">Thomas Reid</a>, <i>Essays on the Intellectual Powers of Man</i>, 1786</cite></div>

####A change is as good as a rest####

####"A <a href="/wiki/Drown" class="mw-redirect" title="Drown">drowning</a> man will clutch at a <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a>". Originates from the 16th century, and refers to a desperate situation (i.e. drowning) and using any chance, even if it is hopeless, to get oneself out of it (i.e. grabbing [clutching] a straw).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>####

####A <a href="/wiki/Friend" class="mw-redirect" title="Friend">friend</a> in need is a <a href="/wiki/Friend" class="mw-redirect" title="Friend">friend</a> indeed<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>####

####A <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopard</a> cannot change its spots####

</ul>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
